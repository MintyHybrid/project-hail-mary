8.1 Background

Potyvirus infection occurs against the backdrop of highly dynamic plant immune receptor trafficking. Understanding how the vesicle trafficking network responds to infection requires integrating three interconnected pathways:

AP-4 complex (Adaptor Protein Complex 4) is a heterotetrameric TGN-resident complex (subunits AP4B, AP4E, AP4M, AP4S) that mediates clathrin-independent trafficking from the trans-Golgi network to endosomes. Unlike AP-1/2/3, AP-4 operates without a clathrin coat. In Arabidopsis it is experimentally established to sort cargo bearing tyrosine-based (YXXΦ) motifs — including VACUOLAR SORTING RECEPTOR1 (VSR1) and the borate transporter BOR1 — via its µ (AP4M) subunit (Fuji et al. 2016), and AP-4 subunit interactions have been mapped systematically (Wang et al. 2022). Its most conserved cargo is ATG9, a lipid scramblase essential for autophagosome biogenesis (Mattera et al. 2017,).

Crucially for immunity, AP-4 is required for the fusion of the vacuolar and plasma membranes that executes the hypersensitive cell-death response to avirulent bacteria: ap4 mutants fail to fuse and lose resistance to effectors recognised at the plasma membrane (Hatsugai et al. 2018), part of the vacuole-membrane fusion immunity first described by Hatsugai et al. (2009; Hara-Nishimura and Hatsugai 2011). And most directly, AP-4 reads a tyrosine motif on an immune protein: Huang et al. (2026) showed that the AP-4 µ subunit binds the transmembrane immunity component BDA1 through BDA1’s tyrosines Y18/Y257, and that AP-4 is required for SNC2-mediated immunity and for basal, pattern- and effector-triggered immunity — a published precedent for a plant defence protein being AP-4-sorted via a YXXΦ-type signal.

By contrast, the assignment of the immune regulators NPR1 and CBP60g as AP-4 cargo — and the idea of AP-4-dependent NPR1 “endosomal processing” before nuclear import — are predictions inferred from mammalian AP-4 biology and sorting-motif analysis, not established plant facts. In particular, no direct experimental AP-4–NPR1 interaction is documented, so no such edge is drawn in the protein-interaction network (Chapter 5); these links are included here only as explicit, testable hypotheses (lower confidence than the ATG9/VSR1 cargo) and are flagged as predicted wherever they appear.

ESCRT pathway (Endosomal Sorting Complexes Required for Transport) mediates ubiquitin-dependent sorting of activated immune receptors (e.g. FLS2, EFR) from early endosomes into intraluminal vesicles (ILVs) of multivesicular bodies (MVBs), ultimately targeting them for vacuolar degradation. ESCRT-I mutation causes FLS2 mis-sorting and attenuated PTI (Spallek et al. 2013). Phosphoinositide PI(3)P on early endosomes and PI(3,5)P₂ on late endosomes/MVBs coordinate ESCRT recruitment and MVB maturation (Simon et al. 2016).

Selective autophagy provides an alternative degradation route and is functionally connected to ESCRT via shared PI(3)P pools and NBR1-mediated cargo recruitment. Autophagy is required for both immune amplification (clearing protein aggregates, eliminating pathogens) and immune termination (receptor turnover) (Üstün et al. 2018; Leary et al. 2018).

The virus works the very same surfaces. This is not a coincidental overlap: positive-strand RNA viruses — potyviruses included — remodel the host endomembrane system into viral replication organelles (VROs), and the same components that run immune trafficking are co-opted to build and protect them (Jovanović et al. 2023). Potyvirus VRO membranes are enriched in PI(3)P and PI(4)P, recruit ATG9 and other autophagy factors, and are shaped by ESCRT and by the proteasomal/lytic pathways — exactly the nodes this model tracks (Jovanović et al. 2023). Concretely, TuMV co-opts the vacuolar sorting receptor VSR4 to target its replication vesicles to the endosome for genome replication (Wu et al. 2022), and its CI protein reaches endosomes via AP-2-read dileucine signals (Chapter 5) — so the viral replication machinery is delivered through precisely the AP-adaptor / PI-lipid / autophagy / ESCRT circuitry modelled below.

The simulation below models steady-state receptor pool dynamics across all three pathways, allowing in silico perturbation of individual components to predict pathological outcomes.

function getPerturbationModifiers(type) {
  const m = {
    endocytosis: 1, ubiquitination: 1, escrt: 1, autophagy: 1,
    recycling: 1, fusion: 1, lipids: 1, ros: 1, cellWall: 1,
    ap4Sorting: 1, npr1Nuclear: 1, cbp60gNuclear: 1, exosomeTopology: 0
  };
  switch (type) {
    case "ap4_knockout":
      // Indirect topology risk only (autophagy→ESCRT stress); AP-4 is not in the
      // scission machinery.
      m.ap4Sorting = 0.05; m.autophagy = 0.6; m.npr1Nuclear = 0.5; m.exosomeTopology = 0.15; break;
    case "escrt_knockout":
      m.escrt = 0.05; m.autophagy = 1.3; m.ros = 1.2; m.exosomeTopology = 0.12; break;
    case "atg5_knockout":
      m.autophagy = 0.05; m.escrt = 1.3; m.ros = 0.9; break;
    case "vps4_dominant_negative":
      // Direct ESCRT-III scission defect — mechanistically the strongest single
      // driver of topology reversal.
      m.escrt = 0.1; m.lipids = 0.6; m.ros = 1.4; m.exosomeTopology = 0.35; break;
    case "bafilomycin":
      // Blocks vacuolar fusion/acidification (degradation), not autophagosome
      // formation → autophagosomes accumulate.
      m.fusion = 0.1; m.escrt = 1.1; break;
    case "pi3k_inhibitor":
      m.escrt = 0.7; m.autophagy = 0.7; m.lipids = 0.5; break;
    case "sterol_depletion":
      m.endocytosis = 0.7; m.escrt = 0.8; m.lipids = 0.3; m.exosomeTopology = 0.18; break;
    case "ap4_escrt_double":
      m.ap4Sorting = 0.05; m.escrt = 0.05; m.autophagy = 0.4; m.ros = 1.5; m.exosomeTopology = 0.45; break;
    case "ap4_dominant_negative":
      // Corrected: same topology risk as AP-4 KO (0.15). A DN sequesters cargo
      // but adds no mechanism for membrane-scission/topology errors beyond the KO
      // (see model-assumptions section).
      m.ap4Sorting = 0.3; m.autophagy = 0.7; m.npr1Nuclear = 0.7; m.exosomeTopology = 0.15; break;
  }
  return m;
}

// Light pathogen-specific modifiers (illustrative). The virus co-opts
// autophagy/endomembrane flux (potyvirus 6K2/VPg), fungi mainly drive ROS.
function getPathogenModifiers(type) {
  const p = { ros: 1, autophagy: 1, escrt: 1 };
  switch (type) {
    case "magnaporthe":    p.ros = 1.3; break;
    case "colletotrichum": p.ros = 1.1; p.autophagy = 0.85; break;
    case "botrytis":       p.ros = 1.5; break;
    case "fusarium":       p.ros = 1.2; break;
    case "potyvirus":      p.autophagy = 1.4; p.escrt = 1.2; break;
  }
  return p;
}

simulation = {
  // Restart cleanly whenever controls change
  const dt = 0.1;
  let s = {
    pm: 100, ee: 0, mvb: 0, auto: 0, vac: 0,
    ros: 0, mapk: 0, callose: 0,
    pi3p_ee: 2.5, pi3p_mvb: 5.0,
    atg9_tgn: 100, npr1_nucleus: 0, cbp60g_nucleus: 0,
    exo_normal: 0, exo_reversed: 0,
    time: 0
  };
  let hist = [];
  let t = 0;

  // Single source of truth: every rate constant the model uses lives here
  // (matches the parameter table in "Model assumptions"). Rates are min⁻¹
  // unless noted; receptor_synthesis is a zero-order replenishment coefficient.
  const params = {
    internalization: 0.30, recycling: 0.20, escrt_recruitment: 0.25,
    mvb_fusion: 0.15, atg_activation: 0.10, autophagy_flux: 0.12,
    pi3p_prod_ee: 0.04, pi3p_prod_mvb: 0.03,
    pi3p_cons_ee: 0.020, pi3p_cons_mvb: 0.030,
    atg9_export: 0.04, atg9_backlog: 2.5,
    npr1_import_pti: 0.12, npr1_import_eti: 0.30, cbp60g_import: 0.15,
    exo_rate: 0.80, exo_normal: 0.04,
    k_ros_decay: 0.513, k_mapk_decay: 0.202,   // exp(-k·dt) reproduces 0.95/0.98 at dt=0.1
    receptor_synthesis: 0.15                   // homeostatic PM replenishment toward baseline
  };
  const PM0 = 100;   // PM baseline that basal resynthesis restores toward

  while (true) {
    if (isRunning && t < timeMax) {
      const mo = getPerturbationModifiers(perturbation.value);
      const pmod = getPathogenModifiers(pathogen.value);
      const rv = response.value;

      // --- First-order mass-action fluxes (each proportional to dt exactly once) ---
      const internalized = s.pm  * params.internalization    * dt * mo.endocytosis;
      const recycled     = s.ee  * params.recycling          * dt * mo.recycling;
      // ESCRT+ubiquitin sorting of endosomal cargo into ILVs of the MVB
      const ilv_form     = s.ee  * params.escrt_recruitment  * dt * mo.escrt * mo.ubiquitination;
      // MVB→vacuole delivery of ILVs
      const mvb_fused    = s.mvb * params.mvb_fusion         * dt * mo.fusion;
      // autophagosome formation (ATG machinery) and autophagosome→vacuole fusion
      const auto_induced = s.ee  * params.atg_activation     * dt * mo.autophagy * pmod.autophagy;
      const auto_flux    = s.auto * params.autophagy_flux    * dt * mo.fusion;

      // --- Signalling (phenomenological, pathogen-modulated). Decay uses
      //     exp(-k·dt) so the rate is dt-independent (reproduces 0.95/0.98 at dt=0.1) ---
      const ros_t   = t < 5  ? Math.exp(-t / 2) * 100 * mo.ros * pmod.ros : s.ros  * Math.exp(-params.k_ros_decay  * dt);
      const mapk_t  = t < 10 ? (1 - Math.exp(-t / 3)) * 80 * mo.ros : s.mapk * Math.exp(-params.k_mapk_decay * dt);
      const cal_t   = t < 20 ? ros_t * 0.3 : s.callose * Math.exp(-params.k_ros_decay * dt);

      // --- PI(3)P: produced on endosomes, consumed by ILV formation (ESCRT) ---
      const pi3p_ee_new  = Math.max(0.05, s.pi3p_ee  + params.pi3p_prod_ee  * dt * mo.lipids - params.pi3p_cons_ee  * ilv_form);
      const pi3p_mvb_new = Math.max(0.05, s.pi3p_mvb + params.pi3p_prod_mvb * dt * mo.lipids - params.pi3p_cons_mvb * ilv_form);

      // --- AP-4 cargo sorting; ATG9 backs up at the TGN when AP-4 is impaired ---
      const atg9_sorted   = s.atg9_tgn * params.atg9_export * dt * mo.ap4Sorting;
      const atg9_backlog  = (1 - mo.ap4Sorting) * params.atg9_backlog * dt;
      const npr1_import   = (rv === "eti" ? params.npr1_import_eti : params.npr1_import_pti) * dt * mo.npr1Nuclear * mo.ap4Sorting;
      const cbp60g_import = params.cbp60g_import * dt * mo.cbp60gNuclear * mo.ap4Sorting;

      // --- Exosomes: reversed-topology fraction = intrinsic perturbation risk,
      //     amplified by membrane stress from CARGO BACKLOG. Keyed off accumulated
      //     cargo (not ESCRT flux or PI(3)P), so aberrant vesiculation under an
      //     ESCRT-deficient background (e.g. ap4+escrt double KO) still produces
      //     reversed exosomes — matching the "aberrant ILV formation without
      //     functional ESCRT" mechanism. ---
      const backlog       = Math.min(2, (s.ee + s.mvb) / 40);   // 0..2 membrane stress
      const exo_rev_gain  = mo.exosomeTopology * (0.5 + backlog) * params.exo_rate * dt;
      const exo_norm_gain = (1 - mo.exosomeTopology) * s.mvb * params.exo_normal * dt;

      // basal receptor resynthesis toward baseline → PM reaches a nonzero steady state
      const pm_synth      = params.receptor_synthesis * Math.max(0, PM0 - s.pm) * dt;

      s = {
        pm:             Math.max(0, s.pm   - internalized + recycled + pm_synth),
        ee:             Math.max(0, s.ee   + internalized - recycled - ilv_form - auto_induced),
        mvb:            Math.max(0, s.mvb  + ilv_form - mvb_fused),
        auto:           Math.max(0, s.auto + auto_induced - auto_flux),
        vac:            s.vac + mvb_fused + auto_flux,
        ros:            Math.max(0, ros_t),
        mapk:           Math.max(0, mapk_t),
        callose:        Math.max(0, cal_t),
        pi3p_ee:        pi3p_ee_new,
        pi3p_mvb:       pi3p_mvb_new,
        atg9_tgn:       Math.max(20, s.atg9_tgn - atg9_sorted + atg9_backlog),
        npr1_nucleus:   Math.max(0, Math.min(50, s.npr1_nucleus + npr1_import)),
        cbp60g_nucleus: Math.max(0, Math.min(30, s.cbp60g_nucleus + cbp60g_import)),
        exo_normal:     Math.max(0, s.exo_normal  + exo_norm_gain),
        exo_reversed:   Math.max(0, s.exo_reversed + exo_rev_gain),
        time: t
      };
      t = parseFloat((t + dt).toFixed(2));

      if (Math.round(t * 10) % 5 === 0) {
        hist = [...hist.slice(-800), { ...s }];
      }
    }

    yield { state: s, history: hist, time: t };
    await Promises.delay(30);
  }
}

pathologies = {
  const s = simState;
  const t = simTime;
  const alerts = [];
  // Failed receptor downregulation (impaired endocytosis) → receptors persist at PM
  if (s.pm > 70 && t > 30)
    alerts.push({ level: "warning", title: "Impaired receptor downregulation", msg: "PM receptors not cleared — risk of chronic signalling" });
  // Endosomal / MVB overload (impaired sorting or fusion)
  if (s.ee > 50 || s.mvb > 50)
    alerts.push({ level: "warning", title: "Endosomal overload", msg: "Cargo accumulating in EE/MVB — impaired sorting or fusion" });
  // Autophagosome accumulation without degradation (blocked fusion, e.g. bafilomycin)
  if (s.auto > 40 && t > 25)
    alerts.push({ level: "warning", title: "Autophagy flux blockade", msg: "Autophagosomes accumulate without vacuolar degradation" });
  // AP-4 deficiency signature: ATG9 backs up at the TGN
  if (s.atg9_tgn > 150 && t > 15)
    alerts.push({ level: "warning", title: "ATG9 accumulation at TGN", msg: "AP-4 deficiency → ATG9 mis-sorting → impaired autophagosome biogenesis" });
  // NPR1 fails to reach the nucleus despite signal (AP-4-dependent, predicted)
  if (s.npr1_nucleus < 3 && s.atg9_tgn > 130 && t > 30)
    alerts.push({ level: "warning", title: "NPR1 nuclear-import failure (predicted)", msg: "AP-4 deficiency → NPR1 mis-sorting → impaired SAR (hypothesis, cross-species inference)" });
  // PI(3)P depletion on MVB (membrane-identity loss; precondition for topology errors)
  if (s.pi3p_mvb < 1.0 && t > 15)
    alerts.push({ level: "warning", title: "PI(3)P depletion (MVB)", msg: "Loss of endosomal identity — ESCRT assembly compromised" });
  // Reversed-topology exosomes — the autoimmune-risk readout
  if (s.exo_reversed > 20)
    alerts.push({ level: "alert", title: "⚠ MEMBRANE TOPOLOGY INVERSION", msg: "Reversed-topology exosomes accumulating — cytoplasmic epitopes exposed → potential autoimmune response" });
  return alerts;
}

8.3 Receptor fate and the PTI/ETI dichotomy

The compartment model above encodes a single decision that recurs throughout plant immunity: once an activated receptor is internalised, is it recycled back to the plasma membrane (sustaining signalling) or committed to degradation (attenuating it)? Ubiquitination is the switch — K63-linked chains route cargo into the ESCRT/MVB pathway, from where it is delivered either to the vacuole (degradation) or, via NBR1, to selective autophagy (Spallek et al. 2013; Üstün et al. 2018).

The two branches of immunity differ systematically in how they use this trafficking machinery. Pattern-triggered immunity (PTI), initiated by surface-localised pattern-recognition receptors (PRRs), is fast and relies heavily on endocytosis and recycling; effector-triggered immunity (ETI), initiated by intracellular NLR receptors, is slower, autophagy-dominant, and culminates in the hypersensitive response (HR):

Feature PTI ETI
Receptors PRRs (plasma membrane) NLRs (cytoplasm)
Activation MAMP/PAMP Effector (direct/indirect)
Kinetics Fast (minutes) Slower (hours)
Ubiquitination PUB12/PUB13 PUB22/PUB23
Trafficking Heavy endocytosis/recycling Autophagy-dominant
ESCRT role Signal attenuation Positive and negative
Autophagy Minor Major (selective degradation)
Output Basal resistance HR + resistance
Amplitude Moderate Strong/hypersensitive
Duration Transient Prolonged
Key adaptors TOM1, VPS23 NBR1, TSPO
Membrane flux High PM turnover Organelle remodelling
Cell-death programme No Yes (PCD/HR)

8.4 Pathogen effector strategies

Because trafficking controls the amplitude and duration of immune signalling, pathogen effectors repeatedly target it. The recurrent strategies fall into six classes — each intercepting a different step of the receptor life-cycle mapped above (effectors are illustrative examples from bacterial and oomycete systems):

# Strategy Example effectors Host targets Mechanism
1 Direct receptor suppression AvrPto, AvrPtoB FLS2, BAK1, CERK1 Block receptor activation or promote its degradation
2 Endocytic disruption HopM1, HopQ1 ARA7 (Rab), RabF2a Prevent endosome maturation/trafficking
3 SNARE inhibition RXLR effectors, HopO1-1 VTI12, SYP61 Block vesicle fusion and recycling
4 Autophagy manipulation AVR-Pik, AvrRps4 NBR1, ATG8 Prevent selective autophagy of NLRs
5 Ubiquitination hijacking AvrPtoB (RING), Avr3a PUB E3 ligases, CMPG1 Act as an E3, or inhibit host ubiquitination
6 Guard-model trigger (ETI) AvrRpm1, AvrRpt2 RIN4 (guard) Modify a guard protein, triggering NLR activation

The evolutionary logic is an arms race: pathogens secrete effectors to suppress PTI at multiple trafficking steps; plants evolve NLRs that detect those effectors (or the modifications they make to guarded host proteins) and mount ETI. Because NLR trafficking itself tunes ETI amplitude, and autophagy plays the dual role of both suppressing PTI and regulating ETI, the balance between degradation and recycling ultimately sets immune homeostasis.

8.5 Model assumptions & calculations

The simulation is a deterministic compartment model integrated by explicit Euler steps (Δt = 0.1 min). State variables are pool sizes (arbitrary units) across compartments: plasma membrane (PM), early endosome (EE), MVB/ILVs, autophagosome, vacuole, TGN (ATG9), nucleus (NPR1, CBP60g) and secreted exosomes (normal vs reversed topology), plus PI(3)P on EE and MVB and the signalling outputs ROS/MAPK/callose.

Kinetics. Transport steps are first-order mass action: each flux is the product of a source pool, a rate constant, and the relevant perturbation (\(m\)) and pathogen (\(p\)) modifiers. Writing the compartment pools as \([\mathrm{PM}], [\mathrm{EE}], [\mathrm{MVB}], [\mathrm{Auto}], [\mathrm{Vac}]\), the core system integrated by explicit Euler (\(\Delta t = 0.1\)) is

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d[\mathrm{PM}]}{dt} &= -k_{\text{int}}\,m_{\text{endo}}\,[\mathrm{PM}] + k_{\text{rec}}\,m_{\text{rec}}\,[\mathrm{EE}] + k_{\text{syn}}\,([\mathrm{PM}]_0 - [\mathrm{PM}])\\[2pt] \frac{d[\mathrm{EE}]}{dt} &= k_{\text{int}}m_{\text{endo}}[\mathrm{PM}] - k_{\text{rec}}m_{\text{rec}}[\mathrm{EE}] - \underbrace{k_{\text{ILV}}m_{\text{escrt}}m_{\text{ub}}[\mathrm{EE}]}_{J_{\text{ILV}}} - k_{\text{atg}}m_{\text{auto}}p_{\text{auto}}[\mathrm{EE}]\\[2pt] \frac{d[\mathrm{MVB}]}{dt} &= J_{\text{ILV}} - k_{\text{fus}}\,m_{\text{fus}}\,[\mathrm{MVB}]\\[2pt] \frac{d[\mathrm{Auto}]}{dt} &= k_{\text{atg}}m_{\text{auto}}p_{\text{auto}}[\mathrm{EE}] - k_{\text{flux}}\,m_{\text{fus}}\,[\mathrm{Auto}]\\[2pt] \frac{d[\mathrm{Vac}]}{dt} &= k_{\text{fus}}m_{\text{fus}}[\mathrm{MVB}] + k_{\text{flux}}m_{\text{fus}}[\mathrm{Auto}]. \end{aligned} \]

ESCRT sorting into ILVs (\(J_{\text{ILV}}\)) requires both ubiquitination and ESCRT function, hence the product \(m_{\text{escrt}}m_{\text{ub}}\). The final \(k_{\text{syn}}([\mathrm{PM}]_0-[\mathrm{PM}])\) term is basal receptor resynthesis: the plasma-membrane pool is replenished toward its baseline \([\mathrm{PM}]_0\), so under balanced synthesis and trafficking PM relaxes to a non-zero steady state rather than draining entirely to the vacuole; perturbations then shift that steady state (the read-out of interest). Rate constants (\(k \approx 0.1\)–\(0.5\ \text{min}^{-1}\); see table) are order-of-magnitude estimates from plant endomembrane/autophagy studies, not fitted to data — absolute values are illustrative and only relative trends are meaningful. Signalling outputs are phenomenological, e.g. \([\mathrm{ROS}](t) = 100\,m_{\text{ros}}p_{\text{ros}}\,e^{-t/2}\) for \(t<5\) min, decaying as \([\mathrm{ROS}]\,e^{-k_{\text{ROS}}\Delta t}\) thereafter (the \(\Delta t\)-explicit form makes the decay rate independent of the step size; MAPK, callose analogous).

PI(3)P coupling. PI(3)P is produced on endosomes and consumed in proportion to ILV formation, with a floor at \(0.05\):

\[ \frac{d[\mathrm{PI3P}]_{\mathrm{EE}}}{dt} = a\,m_{\text{lip}} - b\,J_{\text{ILV}}, \qquad [\mathrm{PI3P}] \ge 0.05, \]

(and analogously on the MVB). Excessive ILV formation therefore depletes PI(3)P, which (a) impairs further ESCRT assembly and (b) raises the membrane-stress term.

AP-4 cargo. ATG9 export and NPR1/CBP60g nuclear import scale with AP-4 function \(m_{\text{ap4}}\); when AP-4 is impaired, ATG9 backs up at the TGN (\(+c\,(1-m_{\text{ap4}})\)) and nuclear import falls: \(\frac{d[\mathrm{NPR1}_n]}{dt} = r_{\text{resp}}\,m_{\text{npr1}}\,m_{\text{ap4}}\), with \(r_{\text{resp}}\) higher under ETI than PTI.

Topology-reversal heuristic. Reversed-topology exosomes accumulate as

\[ \frac{d[\mathrm{exo}_{\text{rev}}]}{dt} = m_{\text{topo}}\,\sigma\,k_{\text{exo}}, \qquad \sigma = 0.5 + \min\!\left(2,\ \frac{[\mathrm{EE}]+[\mathrm{MVB}]}{40}\right), \]

where the intrinsic risk \(m_{\text{topo}}\) is set per perturbation from mechanistic reasoning and the stress factor \(\sigma\) rises with cargo backlog. Crucially this term is decoupled from normal ESCRT flux, so aberrant vesiculation (e.g. the AP-4+ESCRT double KO) still generates reversed exosomes. Intrinsic-risk values:

Perturbation Reversed-topology risk Basis
AP-4 + ESCRT double KO 0.45 Synergistic failure; aberrant ILV formation without ESCRT
VPS4-DN 0.35 Direct ESCRT-III disassembly defect (strongest single driver)
Sterol depletion 0.18 Impaired membrane curvature/scission
AP-4 KO 0.15 Indirect only (autophagy→ESCRT stress)
AP-4-DN 0.15 Same as KO — a DN adds no scission/topology mechanism
ESCRT KO 0.12 Impaired ILV formation

The last two rows encode a deliberate correction: AP-4 is a cargo adaptor, not part of the scission/curvature machinery, so an AP-4 dominant-negative has no mechanistic route to higher topology risk than the knockout — both are set to 0.15, driven indirectly via autophagy→ESCRT stress.

## Background

Potyvirus infection occurs against the backdrop of highly dynamic plant immune
receptor trafficking. Understanding how the vesicle trafficking network responds
to infection requires integrating three interconnected pathways:

**AP-4 complex (Adaptor Protein Complex 4)** is a heterotetrameric TGN-resident
complex (subunits AP4B, AP4E, AP4M, AP4S) that mediates clathrin-independent
trafficking from the *trans*-Golgi network to endosomes. Unlike AP-1/2/3, AP-4
operates without a clathrin coat. In *Arabidopsis* it is experimentally
established to sort cargo bearing tyrosine-based (YXXΦ) motifs — including
**VACUOLAR SORTING RECEPTOR1 (VSR1)** and the borate transporter BOR1 — via its µ
(AP4M) subunit [@fuji2016], and AP-4 subunit interactions have been mapped
systematically [@wang2022ap]. Its most conserved cargo is **ATG9**, a lipid
scramblase essential for autophagosome biogenesis [@mattera2017, ].

Crucially for immunity, AP-4 is required for the **fusion of the vacuolar and
plasma membranes** that executes the hypersensitive cell-death response to
avirulent bacteria: `ap4` mutants fail to fuse and lose resistance to effectors
recognised at the plasma membrane [@hatsugai2018], part of the vacuole-membrane
fusion immunity first described by @hatsugai2009 [@haranishimura2011]. And most
directly, **AP-4 reads a tyrosine motif on an immune protein**: @huang2026 showed
that the AP-4 µ subunit **binds the transmembrane immunity component BDA1 through
BDA1's tyrosines Y18/Y257**, and that AP-4 is required for **SNC2**-mediated
immunity and for basal, pattern- and effector-triggered immunity — a published
precedent for a plant defence protein being AP-4-sorted via a YXXΦ-type signal.

::: {.callout-warning appearance="simple"}
By contrast, the assignment of the immune regulators **NPR1** and **CBP60g** as
AP-4 cargo — and the idea of AP-4-dependent NPR1 "endosomal processing" before
nuclear import — are **predictions inferred from mammalian AP-4 biology and
sorting-motif analysis, not established plant facts**. In particular, **no direct
experimental AP-4–NPR1 interaction is documented**, so no such edge is drawn in
the protein-interaction network (Chapter 5); these links are included here only
as explicit, testable hypotheses (lower confidence than the ATG9/VSR1 cargo) and
are flagged as *predicted* wherever they appear.
:::

**ESCRT pathway** (Endosomal Sorting Complexes Required for Transport)
mediates ubiquitin-dependent sorting of activated immune receptors (e.g. FLS2,
EFR) from early endosomes into intraluminal vesicles (ILVs) of multivesicular
bodies (MVBs), ultimately targeting them for vacuolar degradation. ESCRT-I
mutation causes FLS2 mis-sorting and attenuated PTI [@spallek2013]. Phosphoinositide
PI(3)P on early endosomes and PI(3,5)P₂ on late endosomes/MVBs coordinate ESCRT
recruitment and MVB maturation [@simon2016].

**Selective autophagy** provides an alternative degradation route and is
functionally connected to ESCRT via shared PI(3)P pools and NBR1-mediated
cargo recruitment. Autophagy is required for both immune amplification (clearing
protein aggregates, eliminating pathogens) and immune termination (receptor
turnover) [@ustun2018; @leary2018].

**The virus works the very same surfaces.** This is not a coincidental overlap:
positive-strand RNA viruses — potyviruses included — **remodel the host
endomembrane system into viral replication organelles (VROs)**, and the same
components that run immune trafficking are co-opted to build and protect them
[@jovanovic2023]. Potyvirus VRO membranes are **enriched in PI(3)P and PI(4)P**,
recruit **ATG9 and other autophagy factors**, and are shaped by **ESCRT** and by
the proteasomal/lytic pathways — exactly the nodes this model tracks
[@jovanovic2023]. Concretely, TuMV **co-opts the vacuolar sorting receptor VSR4 to
target its replication vesicles to the endosome** for genome replication
[@wu2022vsr4], and its CI protein reaches endosomes via **AP-2-read dileucine
signals** (Chapter 5) — so the viral replication machinery is delivered through
precisely the AP-adaptor / PI-lipid / autophagy / ESCRT circuitry modelled below.

The simulation below models steady-state receptor pool dynamics across all three
pathways, allowing in silico perturbation of individual components to predict
pathological outcomes.

```{ojs}
simulation = {
  // Restart cleanly whenever controls change
  const dt = 0.1;
  let s = {
    pm: 100, ee: 0, mvb: 0, auto: 0, vac: 0,
    ros: 0, mapk: 0, callose: 0,
    pi3p_ee: 2.5, pi3p_mvb: 5.0,
    atg9_tgn: 100, npr1_nucleus: 0, cbp60g_nucleus: 0,
    exo_normal: 0, exo_reversed: 0,
    time: 0
  };
  let hist = [];
  let t = 0;

  // Single source of truth: every rate constant the model uses lives here
  // (matches the parameter table in "Model assumptions"). Rates are min⁻¹
  // unless noted; receptor_synthesis is a zero-order replenishment coefficient.
  const params = {
    internalization: 0.30, recycling: 0.20, escrt_recruitment: 0.25,
    mvb_fusion: 0.15, atg_activation: 0.10, autophagy_flux: 0.12,
    pi3p_prod_ee: 0.04, pi3p_prod_mvb: 0.03,
    pi3p_cons_ee: 0.020, pi3p_cons_mvb: 0.030,
    atg9_export: 0.04, atg9_backlog: 2.5,
    npr1_import_pti: 0.12, npr1_import_eti: 0.30, cbp60g_import: 0.15,
    exo_rate: 0.80, exo_normal: 0.04,
    k_ros_decay: 0.513, k_mapk_decay: 0.202,   // exp(-k·dt) reproduces 0.95/0.98 at dt=0.1
    receptor_synthesis: 0.15                   // homeostatic PM replenishment toward baseline
  };
  const PM0 = 100;   // PM baseline that basal resynthesis restores toward

  while (true) {
    if (isRunning && t < timeMax) {
      const mo = getPerturbationModifiers(perturbation.value);
      const pmod = getPathogenModifiers(pathogen.value);
      const rv = response.value;

      // --- First-order mass-action fluxes (each proportional to dt exactly once) ---
      const internalized = s.pm  * params.internalization    * dt * mo.endocytosis;
      const recycled     = s.ee  * params.recycling          * dt * mo.recycling;
      // ESCRT+ubiquitin sorting of endosomal cargo into ILVs of the MVB
      const ilv_form     = s.ee  * params.escrt_recruitment  * dt * mo.escrt * mo.ubiquitination;
      // MVB→vacuole delivery of ILVs
      const mvb_fused    = s.mvb * params.mvb_fusion         * dt * mo.fusion;
      // autophagosome formation (ATG machinery) and autophagosome→vacuole fusion
      const auto_induced = s.ee  * params.atg_activation     * dt * mo.autophagy * pmod.autophagy;
      const auto_flux    = s.auto * params.autophagy_flux    * dt * mo.fusion;

      // --- Signalling (phenomenological, pathogen-modulated). Decay uses
      //     exp(-k·dt) so the rate is dt-independent (reproduces 0.95/0.98 at dt=0.1) ---
      const ros_t   = t < 5  ? Math.exp(-t / 2) * 100 * mo.ros * pmod.ros : s.ros  * Math.exp(-params.k_ros_decay  * dt);
      const mapk_t  = t < 10 ? (1 - Math.exp(-t / 3)) * 80 * mo.ros : s.mapk * Math.exp(-params.k_mapk_decay * dt);
      const cal_t   = t < 20 ? ros_t * 0.3 : s.callose * Math.exp(-params.k_ros_decay * dt);

      // --- PI(3)P: produced on endosomes, consumed by ILV formation (ESCRT) ---
      const pi3p_ee_new  = Math.max(0.05, s.pi3p_ee  + params.pi3p_prod_ee  * dt * mo.lipids - params.pi3p_cons_ee  * ilv_form);
      const pi3p_mvb_new = Math.max(0.05, s.pi3p_mvb + params.pi3p_prod_mvb * dt * mo.lipids - params.pi3p_cons_mvb * ilv_form);

      // --- AP-4 cargo sorting; ATG9 backs up at the TGN when AP-4 is impaired ---
      const atg9_sorted   = s.atg9_tgn * params.atg9_export * dt * mo.ap4Sorting;
      const atg9_backlog  = (1 - mo.ap4Sorting) * params.atg9_backlog * dt;
      const npr1_import   = (rv === "eti" ? params.npr1_import_eti : params.npr1_import_pti) * dt * mo.npr1Nuclear * mo.ap4Sorting;
      const cbp60g_import = params.cbp60g_import * dt * mo.cbp60gNuclear * mo.ap4Sorting;

      // --- Exosomes: reversed-topology fraction = intrinsic perturbation risk,
      //     amplified by membrane stress from CARGO BACKLOG. Keyed off accumulated
      //     cargo (not ESCRT flux or PI(3)P), so aberrant vesiculation under an
      //     ESCRT-deficient background (e.g. ap4+escrt double KO) still produces
      //     reversed exosomes — matching the "aberrant ILV formation without
      //     functional ESCRT" mechanism. ---
      const backlog       = Math.min(2, (s.ee + s.mvb) / 40);   // 0..2 membrane stress
      const exo_rev_gain  = mo.exosomeTopology * (0.5 + backlog) * params.exo_rate * dt;
      const exo_norm_gain = (1 - mo.exosomeTopology) * s.mvb * params.exo_normal * dt;

      // basal receptor resynthesis toward baseline → PM reaches a nonzero steady state
      const pm_synth      = params.receptor_synthesis * Math.max(0, PM0 - s.pm) * dt;

      s = {
        pm:             Math.max(0, s.pm   - internalized + recycled + pm_synth),
        ee:             Math.max(0, s.ee   + internalized - recycled - ilv_form - auto_induced),
        mvb:            Math.max(0, s.mvb  + ilv_form - mvb_fused),
        auto:           Math.max(0, s.auto + auto_induced - auto_flux),
        vac:            s.vac + mvb_fused + auto_flux,
        ros:            Math.max(0, ros_t),
        mapk:           Math.max(0, mapk_t),
        callose:        Math.max(0, cal_t),
        pi3p_ee:        pi3p_ee_new,
        pi3p_mvb:       pi3p_mvb_new,
        atg9_tgn:       Math.max(20, s.atg9_tgn - atg9_sorted + atg9_backlog),
        npr1_nucleus:   Math.max(0, Math.min(50, s.npr1_nucleus + npr1_import)),
        cbp60g_nucleus: Math.max(0, Math.min(30, s.cbp60g_nucleus + cbp60g_import)),
        exo_normal:     Math.max(0, s.exo_normal  + exo_norm_gain),
        exo_reversed:   Math.max(0, s.exo_reversed + exo_rev_gain),
        time: t
      };
      t = parseFloat((t + dt).toFixed(2));

      if (Math.round(t * 10) % 5 === 0) {
        hist = [...hist.slice(-800), { ...s }];
      }
    }

    yield { state: s, history: hist, time: t };
    await Promises.delay(30);
  }
}
```

## Receptor fate and the PTI/ETI dichotomy

The compartment model above encodes a single decision that recurs throughout
plant immunity: once an activated receptor is internalised, is it **recycled**
back to the plasma membrane (sustaining signalling) or **committed to
degradation** (attenuating it)? Ubiquitination is the switch — K63-linked chains
route cargo into the ESCRT/MVB pathway, from where it is delivered either to the
vacuole (degradation) or, via NBR1, to selective autophagy [@spallek2013;
@ustun2018].


The two branches of immunity differ systematically in how they use this
trafficking machinery. Pattern-triggered immunity (PTI), initiated by
surface-localised pattern-recognition receptors (PRRs), is fast and relies
heavily on endocytosis and recycling; effector-triggered immunity (ETI),
initiated by intracellular NLR receptors, is slower, autophagy-dominant, and
culminates in the hypersensitive response (HR):

| Feature | PTI | ETI |
|---|---|---|
| Receptors | PRRs (plasma membrane) | NLRs (cytoplasm) |
| Activation | MAMP/PAMP | Effector (direct/indirect) |
| Kinetics | Fast (minutes) | Slower (hours) |
| Ubiquitination | PUB12/PUB13 | PUB22/PUB23 |
| Trafficking | Heavy endocytosis/recycling | Autophagy-dominant |
| ESCRT role | Signal attenuation | Positive *and* negative |
| Autophagy | Minor | Major (selective degradation) |
| Output | Basal resistance | HR + resistance |
| Amplitude | Moderate | Strong/hypersensitive |
| Duration | Transient | Prolonged |
| Key adaptors | TOM1, VPS23 | NBR1, TSPO |
| Membrane flux | High PM turnover | Organelle remodelling |
| Cell-death programme | No | Yes (PCD/HR) |

## Pathogen effector strategies

Because trafficking controls the amplitude and duration of immune signalling,
pathogen effectors repeatedly target it. The recurrent strategies fall into six
classes — each intercepting a different step of the receptor life-cycle mapped
above (effectors are illustrative examples from bacterial and oomycete systems):

| # | Strategy | Example effectors | Host targets | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Direct receptor suppression | AvrPto, AvrPtoB | FLS2, BAK1, CERK1 | Block receptor activation or promote its degradation |
| 2 | Endocytic disruption | HopM1, HopQ1 | ARA7 (Rab), RabF2a | Prevent endosome maturation/trafficking |
| 3 | SNARE inhibition | RXLR effectors, HopO1-1 | VTI12, SYP61 | Block vesicle fusion and recycling |
| 4 | Autophagy manipulation | AVR-Pik, AvrRps4 | NBR1, ATG8 | Prevent selective autophagy of NLRs |
| 5 | Ubiquitination hijacking | AvrPtoB (RING), Avr3a | PUB E3 ligases, CMPG1 | Act as an E3, or inhibit host ubiquitination |
| 6 | Guard-model trigger (ETI) | AvrRpm1, AvrRpt2 | RIN4 (guard) | Modify a guard protein, triggering NLR activation |

The evolutionary logic is an arms race: pathogens secrete effectors to suppress
PTI at multiple trafficking steps; plants evolve NLRs that detect those effectors
(or the modifications they make to guarded host proteins) and mount ETI. Because
NLR trafficking itself tunes ETI amplitude, and autophagy plays the dual role of
both suppressing PTI and regulating ETI, the balance between degradation and
recycling ultimately sets immune homeostasis.

## Model assumptions & calculations

The simulation is a **deterministic compartment model** integrated by explicit
Euler steps (Δt = 0.1 min). State variables are pool sizes (arbitrary units)
across compartments: plasma membrane (PM), early endosome (EE), MVB/ILVs,
autophagosome, vacuole, TGN (ATG9), nucleus (NPR1, CBP60g) and secreted exosomes
(normal vs reversed topology), plus PI(3)P on EE and MVB and the signalling
outputs ROS/MAPK/callose.

**Kinetics.** Transport steps are **first-order mass action**: each flux is the
product of a source pool, a rate constant, and the relevant perturbation ($m$)
and pathogen ($p$) modifiers. Writing the compartment pools as
$[\mathrm{PM}], [\mathrm{EE}], [\mathrm{MVB}], [\mathrm{Auto}], [\mathrm{Vac}]$,
the core system integrated by explicit Euler ($\Delta t = 0.1$) is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d[\mathrm{PM}]}{dt}   &= -k_{\text{int}}\,m_{\text{endo}}\,[\mathrm{PM}] + k_{\text{rec}}\,m_{\text{rec}}\,[\mathrm{EE}] + k_{\text{syn}}\,([\mathrm{PM}]_0 - [\mathrm{PM}])\\[2pt]
\frac{d[\mathrm{EE}]}{dt}   &= k_{\text{int}}m_{\text{endo}}[\mathrm{PM}] - k_{\text{rec}}m_{\text{rec}}[\mathrm{EE}] - \underbrace{k_{\text{ILV}}m_{\text{escrt}}m_{\text{ub}}[\mathrm{EE}]}_{J_{\text{ILV}}} - k_{\text{atg}}m_{\text{auto}}p_{\text{auto}}[\mathrm{EE}]\\[2pt]
\frac{d[\mathrm{MVB}]}{dt}  &= J_{\text{ILV}} - k_{\text{fus}}\,m_{\text{fus}}\,[\mathrm{MVB}]\\[2pt]
\frac{d[\mathrm{Auto}]}{dt} &= k_{\text{atg}}m_{\text{auto}}p_{\text{auto}}[\mathrm{EE}] - k_{\text{flux}}\,m_{\text{fus}}\,[\mathrm{Auto}]\\[2pt]
\frac{d[\mathrm{Vac}]}{dt}  &= k_{\text{fus}}m_{\text{fus}}[\mathrm{MVB}] + k_{\text{flux}}m_{\text{fus}}[\mathrm{Auto}].
\end{aligned}
$$

ESCRT sorting into ILVs ($J_{\text{ILV}}$) requires **both** ubiquitination and
ESCRT function, hence the product $m_{\text{escrt}}m_{\text{ub}}$. The final
$k_{\text{syn}}([\mathrm{PM}]_0-[\mathrm{PM}])$ term is **basal receptor
resynthesis**: the plasma-membrane pool is replenished toward its baseline
$[\mathrm{PM}]_0$, so under balanced synthesis and trafficking PM relaxes to a
**non-zero steady state** rather than draining entirely to the vacuole;
perturbations then shift that steady state (the read-out of interest). Rate
constants ($k \approx 0.1$–$0.5\ \text{min}^{-1}$; see table) are
**order-of-magnitude estimates** from plant endomembrane/autophagy studies,
**not fitted to data** — absolute values are illustrative and only relative
trends are meaningful. Signalling outputs are phenomenological, e.g.
$[\mathrm{ROS}](t) = 100\,m_{\text{ros}}p_{\text{ros}}\,e^{-t/2}$ for $t<5$ min,
decaying as $[\mathrm{ROS}]\,e^{-k_{\text{ROS}}\Delta t}$ thereafter (the
$\Delta t$-explicit form makes the decay rate independent of the step size;
MAPK, callose analogous).

**PI(3)P coupling.** PI(3)P is produced on endosomes and **consumed in proportion
to ILV formation**, with a floor at $0.05$:

$$
\frac{d[\mathrm{PI3P}]_{\mathrm{EE}}}{dt} = a\,m_{\text{lip}} - b\,J_{\text{ILV}},
\qquad [\mathrm{PI3P}] \ge 0.05,
$$

(and analogously on the MVB). Excessive ILV formation therefore depletes PI(3)P,
which (a) impairs further ESCRT assembly and (b) raises the membrane-stress term.

**AP-4 cargo.** ATG9 export and NPR1/CBP60g nuclear import scale with AP-4
function $m_{\text{ap4}}$; when AP-4 is impaired, ATG9 backs up at the TGN
($+c\,(1-m_{\text{ap4}})$) and nuclear import falls:
$\frac{d[\mathrm{NPR1}_n]}{dt} = r_{\text{resp}}\,m_{\text{npr1}}\,m_{\text{ap4}}$,
with $r_{\text{resp}}$ higher under ETI than PTI.

**Topology-reversal heuristic.** Reversed-topology exosomes accumulate as

$$
\frac{d[\mathrm{exo}_{\text{rev}}]}{dt} = m_{\text{topo}}\,\sigma\,k_{\text{exo}},
\qquad
\sigma = 0.5 + \min\!\left(2,\ \frac{[\mathrm{EE}]+[\mathrm{MVB}]}{40}\right),
$$

where the *intrinsic risk* $m_{\text{topo}}$ is set per perturbation from
mechanistic reasoning and the *stress* factor $\sigma$ rises with cargo backlog.
Crucially this term is **decoupled from normal ESCRT flux**, so aberrant
vesiculation (e.g. the AP-4+ESCRT double KO) still generates reversed exosomes.
Intrinsic-risk values:

| Perturbation | Reversed-topology risk | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| AP-4 + ESCRT double KO | 0.45 | Synergistic failure; aberrant ILV formation without ESCRT |
| VPS4-DN | 0.35 | Direct ESCRT-III disassembly defect (strongest single driver) |
| Sterol depletion | 0.18 | Impaired membrane curvature/scission |
| AP-4 KO | 0.15 | **Indirect only** (autophagy→ESCRT stress) |
| AP-4-DN | 0.15 | **Same as KO** — a DN adds no scission/topology mechanism |
| ESCRT KO | 0.12 | Impaired ILV formation |

The last two rows encode a deliberate correction: **AP-4 is a cargo adaptor, not
part of the scission/curvature machinery**, so an AP-4 dominant-negative has no
mechanistic route to *higher* topology risk than the knockout — both are set to
0.15, driven indirectly via autophagy→ESCRT stress.
